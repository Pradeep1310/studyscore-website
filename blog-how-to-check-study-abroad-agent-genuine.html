How to Check If Your Study Abroad Agent Is Genuine: A 10-Point Checklist

I've spent 18 years on the inside of this industry. I've managed 7,000+ education agents across 40 countries. Here are the 10 things I'd check before letting my own family sign with an agent.

PP
Pradeep Pandey
Founder, StudyScore · 18 years in International Education · April 9, 2026

Let me tell you something that might be hard to hear.

In my 18 years in international education — at ApplyBoard, Google, StudyGroup, and consulting across 40+ countries — I've onboarded and worked with over 7,000 education agents. The overwhelming majority are decent, hardworking professionals trying to help students get abroad. But a significant minority are not. And from the outside, sitting in an agent's office in Jalandhar or Kota or Hyderabad, you cannot tell the difference.

The good agent and the bad agent look exactly the same. Same office. Same wall of visa approval letters. Same Instagram with smiling students at airports. Same "99% success rate" on the website. Same confident pitch: "Aap fikar mat karo, sab ho jayega."

The difference shows up later. After you've paid. After the SOP turns out to be copy-pasted. After the university they recommended rejects your application because your profile didn't match. After the visa comes through and suddenly nobody picks up your calls about accommodation and airport pickup. By then, your money is gone and your options are limited.

This article is for families who are about to make that ₹15-40 lakh decision. I'm writing the checklist I wish I could hand to every parent sitting across from an agent's desk, trying to decide whether to sign.

A note before we begin: This checklist is not about catching criminals. Most agents aren't frauds — they're just mediocre. And mediocre, when you're spending your life savings, is not good enough. These 10 checks help you distinguish between an agent who will treat your child's application like it matters and one who will treat it like paperwork to be processed.

The 10 Checks

1

Ask for five past students' phone numbers. Not testimonials. Phone numbers.

This is the single most revealing test. Any confident agent will happily share 5 contacts of students they've sent abroad in the last 12 months. If they hesitate, deflect, or say "privacy policy doesn't allow" — that tells you everything.

When you call those students, ask specific questions: Did the agent disclose all fees upfront? How was communication during the visa process? Did they help after the visa was approved? Would you use them again? The answers from real students are worth more than any Google review.

Red flag: "We can't share student contacts due to confidentiality." Legitimate agents have dozens of happy students who'd gladly recommend them. If they can't produce five, ask yourself why.

Green flag: The agent proactively offers to connect you with past students, including ones who went to the same country or university you're considering.

2

Get the fee structure in writing before you sign anything. Every rupee.

This is where most complaints begin. The agent says "total cost is ₹50,000." You pay. Then three weeks later, there's a "documentation fee." Then a "university application fee." Then a "visa filing charge." Then "courier and attestation charges." By the time you're done, you've paid ₹1.5-2 lakh, not the ₹50,000 you were quoted.

Before you sign, ask for a written document — printed, signed, on company letterhead — listing every single charge you will pay at every stage of the process. Counselling fee, application processing fee, visa filing fee, post-visa service fee, and any other charge. If they can't produce this document, don't sign.

Red flag: "Fees depend on the case" or "we'll discuss charges as we go." No. Fees should be clear before you commit. A legitimate agent knows exactly what they charge because they've done this hundreds of times.

Green flag: A printed fee schedule with stage-wise breakdown, GST details, refund policy, and the agent's signature. Even better if it's part of a formal service agreement.

3

Check their registration — but understand what it actually means.

Agents can be registered with several bodies: AAERI (Association of Australian Education Representatives in India) for Australia-focused agents, ICEF for globally certified agents, AIRC (American International Recruitment Council) for US-focused agents, or the British Council's EATC programme for UK-focused agents. Some states in India also require consultancy registration.

Here's the nuance most people miss: registration doesn't guarantee quality. It means the agent met minimum standards and paid a registration fee. Think of it like a driving licence — having one doesn't make you a good driver. It just means you passed a basic test. But NOT having a licence? That's a clear warning sign.

Verify their registration by checking the relevant body's website. AAERI has a member directory. ICEF has a searchable database. If an agent claims certification, ask for the certificate number and verify it yourself. It takes two minutes.

Red flag: Claims to be "government approved" without specifying which government, which department, or which approval number. In India, there's no single "government approval" for education agents.

4

Ask them to name three universities they'd recommend for YOUR profile — and explain why each one.

A good agent doesn't recommend universities at random. They match YOUR academic background, budget, career goals, and country preference to specific universities that make sense for YOUR profile.

When you ask "which universities would you recommend for me?", listen carefully to the answer. A strong agent will say something like: "Based on your 70% aggregate and budget of ₹18 lakh per year, I'd recommend University A because their admission requirement is 65%, University B because they offer a scholarship for your profile type, and University C as a safety option with a high acceptance rate."

A weak agent will say: "We work with many universities. We'll find the best one for you." That's not a recommendation — that's a deflection. It usually means they'll send your application to whichever universities pay them the highest commission, not the ones best suited for you.

Red flag: The agent pushes one specific university aggressively. In my experience, this often means that university pays the highest commission. A good agent gives you options, not orders.

Green flag: The agent asks you detailed questions about your academic background, career goals, and budget BEFORE recommending universities. They explain the admission requirements, fees, and career outcomes for each recommendation.

5

Ask who writes the SOP. Then ask to see a sample.

The Statement of Purpose is the most important document in your application. It's what the admissions committee reads to decide if you're a genuine, motivated student. At many agencies, SOPs are written by junior staff using templates, with your name and details swapped in. I've seen SOPs where the student's name was wrong because someone forgot to change it from the previous applicant's template.

Ask the agent directly: "Who will write my SOP? Can I see a sample of an SOP you've written for a student with a similar profile?" A good agent will either write it themselves, have a dedicated SOP specialist, or work with you on it collaboratively. The key word is personalised. Your SOP should sound like YOU, not like a template.

Red flag: "Don't worry about the SOP, we handle everything." The SOP is YOUR story. If the agent doesn't involve you in the process, they're writing a generic template that could get your application flagged or rejected.

6

Google them. But don't trust Google Reviews blindly.

Here's an uncomfortable truth from inside the industry: many agents systematically manufacture Google Reviews. They ask every student who gets a visa to post a 5-star review. Some agents offer discounts or "referral bonuses" for reviews. Some have staff post reviews under fake names. I've personally seen agencies with 4.8-star ratings on Google that have terrible actual student satisfaction.

So what should you look for instead? Check the negative reviews. Positive reviews can be faked, but negative reviews are almost always real — because no agent asks someone to post a 1-star review. Read the 1-star and 2-star reviews carefully. Are there patterns? Multiple students mentioning hidden fees? Complaints about post-visa support? That's the real data.

Also check: Do they have a website with actual office address? Can you find the founder/owner on LinkedIn? How long has the company been registered? (Check MCA portal for company registration date.) Do they have an active social media presence with real student content, not just stock images?

The data is aggregated across hundreds of responses, making it much harder to manipulate.

7

Ask about their refund policy. In writing. Before you pay.

What happens if your visa gets rejected? What happens if you change your mind? What happens if the university rejects your application? These are not hypothetical questions — they happen every day.

A legitimate agent will have a clear, written refund policy. It might say: "Full refund minus ₹5,000 administrative charge if visa is rejected" or "50% refund if you cancel before application submission." The specific terms matter less than the fact that they EXIST and are in writing.

Agents who refuse to discuss refund terms before payment are planning to keep your money regardless of outcome. It's that simple.

Red flag: "Refund? Let's not think about negative things. Your visa will definitely be approved." Anyone who guarantees a visa outcome is either lying or doesn't understand how visa processing works. No agent controls the visa decision — that's the embassy's or high commission's job.

8

Ask what happens AFTER the visa. This separates the professionals from the processors.

Getting the visa is the halfway point, not the finish line. After the visa, a student needs: pre-departure briefing (what to pack, what to expect, cultural orientation), accommodation assistance (not just a list of websites, but actual help booking a room), airport pickup coordination, bank account opening guidance, local SIM and transport orientation, and someone to call when things go wrong in the first month.

Most agents consider their job done the moment the visa is stamped. The commission is earned, the next student is waiting, and the one who just got their visa is yesterday's priority. This is the single biggest complaint students have — not fraud, not incompetence, just abandonment.

Ask specifically: "What support do you provide after the visa is approved? Do you help with accommodation? Airport pickup? Is there a contact number I can call after I land?" Get the answers in writing as part of your service agreement.

Green flag: The agent has a structured pre-departure checklist, connects you with a WhatsApp group of students going to the same city, and has a dedicated post-visa support person (not the same counsellor juggling 50 new students).

9

Visit the office. Unannounced.

This sounds old-school, but it works. Call the office and say you want to visit. Then show up a day earlier than the scheduled appointment. Why? Because the scheduled visit is a performance — the office will be clean, the counsellor will be prepared, and everything will look perfect.

The unannounced visit shows you reality. How busy is the office? Are there actual students there, or is it empty? Does the staff seem organised? Is there a reception, a waiting area, a counselling room? Or is it a one-room setup with a single desk? None of these are automatic disqualifiers, but they give you a sense of the operation's scale and professionalism.

Also pay attention to how they treat you when you show up without an appointment. A good office will still welcome you, offer a brief chat, and schedule a detailed session. A bad one will be annoyed or flustered. How they handle the unexpected tells you how they'll handle your unexpected visa complication.

10

Trust your instinct. If something feels off, it probably is.

After 18 years in this industry, I can tell you that the families who avoided bad agents weren't smarter or luckier. They were the ones who paid attention to the small signals. The agent who was too eager to close the deal on the first visit. The counsellor who couldn't answer basic questions about the country's visa process. The office that pressured you with "this discount is only available today." The contract that was suspiciously short on details.

You are about to make a decision worth ₹15-40 lakh of your family's money. You are entitled to take your time. A good agent will wait for your decision. A bad one will pressure you into making it today. That pressure, in itself, is the biggest red flag of all.

My personal rule: If an agent spends more time talking about their success rate than asking about YOUR goals, background, and concerns — walk away. Good agents listen first. Bad agents sell first.

Frequently Asked Questions

How can I verify if a study abroad agent is registered?

Check the relevant certification body's website: AAERI for Australia-focused agents, AIRC for US-focused agents, or ICEF's database for globally certified agents. You can also verify company registration on India's MCA portal. Registration doesn't guarantee quality, but the absence of any registration is a definite red flag.

What are the biggest red flags when choosing a study abroad agent?

Guaranteeing visa approval (no agent can do this), refusing to provide a written fee breakdown, pressuring you to sign immediately, recommending only one university without explaining why, being unable to name specific university contacts, and having no verifiable track record of past students.

Should I pay a study abroad agent the full fee upfront?

Never. A legitimate agent has a staged payment structure: a registration or counselling fee upfront (typically ₹5,000-15,000), application processing fees at submission, and the balance at visa filing. Full upfront payment with no written refund policy is a serious warning sign.

Do good study abroad agents charge fees or work on commission from universities?

Both models are legitimate. Some agents charge students directly, some earn commission from universities (making their service free for students), and some do both. The key is complete transparency about the fee structure in writing before you commit.

How can I check genuine reviews of a study abroad agent?

Don't rely on Google Reviews alone — they can be manipulated. Ask the agent for 3-5 past student contacts to speak with directly. Look for video testimonials on social media (harder to fake).
